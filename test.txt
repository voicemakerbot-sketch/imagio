

“Sounds good. I’ll keep that in mind,” he said.

As I walked out of that building, I had a weird smile on my face.
Part nerves, part relief.
I’d taken a step toward the truth.
But I knew—those results could blow my entire marriage apart.

That thing I said to Jenna about buying more cars? That was just a cover for the storm brewing in my head.
But funny thing is, the idea started to grow on me.
Maybe expanding wasn’t such a bad idea after all.

Back at the dealership, I called in Greg—my accountant, loan officer, and occasional sales guy.

“We’re going to make this work,” I told him confidently. “But I’ve got another idea. I want to pay off the mortgage on the house. Can we pull together a lump sum?”

“Let me crunch the numbers and get back to you after lunch,” he said, then walked out with his tablet.

As he left, I sat there thinking about whether I should call a lawyer. Maybe ask for a referral in family law.

But I hesitated.
“Easy there,” I told myself.
“Let’s wait and see what the test says.”

So far, I still didn’t have hard proof of an affair.

Greg came back after lunch with a spreadsheet and we went over both plans—the business side and the personal.

Then came the waiting.
Two whole days, dragging by like molasses.
Every hour felt like a lifetime as I waited for the DNA results that could change everything.

Late Wednesday evening, the department head from the university gave me a call. He said the results were in and that he’d stop by to go over them—and maybe talk about that car he’d been eyeing. When he showed up at my office, I could tell by the look on his face that the news wasn’t good, even before he said a word.

“The samples aren’t related,” he told me gently.

I just nodded. As much as it stung, I’d been bracing for it. Still, hearing it out loud hit like a truck. He gave me a second to catch my breath, then handed me the envelope with the official results.

When I finally looked up, I straightened my face and switched gears. “So, about that car you were thinking about?” I asked, trying to shift the focus.

He chuckled. “It might sound like a cliché, but I just turned the big five-oh. Figured it’s time for a sports car.”

He said he had his eye on a Mazda Miata. I didn’t have one on the lot, but I promised I’d search the auction the next day and track down a clean one for him.

“I’ll call you this afternoon with any updates,” I told him as I walked him out.

He mentioned telling his assistant, Maya, that he might be unreachable, but I knew she’d manage fine. She rarely needed help.

After he left, I hopped in my truck and drove aimlessly around the city. Everything looked different now, like the whole place had shifted. I finally made the call to my lawyer—broke the news. He was sympathetic but told me he'd connect me with someone who handled family law.

Later that night, over a greasy burger and a side of fries, I found myself wondering… Had I really thrown away fifteen years of my life? Not only did I lose my wife, but I didn’t even have a child to hold onto from the marriage. Sure, I still felt something for Chloe Dalton—always would—but now I had to face the truth: she wasn’t mine. Not by blood.

I couldn’t ignore it anymore. The signs were always there, but now the facts were written in black and white. There was no going back.

One thing was crystal clear—George was going to pay.
Didn’t matter that he’d been my best friend. Hell, with friends like him, who needs enemies? And Jenna? She wasn’t getting off easy either. Whether or not she knew Chloe Dalton wasn’t mine, she’d hidden it all the same.

There was betrayal from someone I trusted deeply—on the most important day of our lives.
And not just once. Based on what I overheard, she was still hooking up with him twice a month.
The betrayal burned deep. I felt this rage bubble up inside me, and I knew I had to step away before I did something I’d regret.

I should’ve called her and let her know I wouldn’t be home. Just common courtesy. But going home? That wasn’t happening.
I was scared of what I might say—or worse, do.

So I called. She picked up. “Hello, Cole.”

“Hey,” I said, my voice flat. “I’m gonna stay in Albuquerque tonight. Got an early auction in the morning.”

“Well, okay then. Drive safe.”

It wasn’t unusual for me to hit up auctions, but Albuquerque was out of the ordinary. I couldn’t help but wonder—would she take advantage of my absence? If so, how would she explain it to Chloe Dalton? Or my folks, if they found out?

Still, I kept checking her phone tracker throughout the night. She stayed put. Or at least, her phone did.

My plan was to buy around twenty cars, including that Miata, and have them hauled back to the lot after the sale.
The real issue? Who could I trust to inspect them?

George was out. No way I’d keep him in my circle after this. He didn’t know it yet, but our business relationship was over the moment he finished his current repairs.

He’d probably see a big haul like this as a payday and try to jack up his rates. But that wasn’t gonna happen.

I kept everything quiet for now. Instead, I planned to talk with a few folks at the auction to find a reliable mechanic in the area who could inspect the cars before they shipped out to my small-town lot.

I rolled into Albuquerque around 9 a.m. and checked into a Best Western.
Nothing fancy, but it was clean and had a comfortable bed.
I don’t need much—just the basics. No need to burn through company money on a luxury suite. I’ve always been a practical guy.

First thing I did after dropping my bag? Pulled up the tracker. Jenna’s phone was still at the house.
Didn’t mean she was there—but at least her phone hadn’t moved.

Sleep didn’t come easy that night. I just couldn’t stop thinking.

How could she?
How could Jenna cheat on me, especially on the day we said our vows?
And how did she justify continuing the affair for all these years?

I started reevaluating everything—our whole marriage.
It was never perfect, sure, but I believed in us. I thought our love was real.
Turns out, I was wrong from the very start.

The early years were rocky. We didn’t plan on having a kid right away.
Our agreement was to wait three or four years, get settled first.
We’d even talked about having three kids total before we tied the knot.

But then Chloe Dalton came along, and everything changed.
Now I knew why. Because of George.

He upended everything.
Every struggle, every sleepless night—suddenly it all felt like it had been for nothing.

Fourteen years of building a life together… washed away by one betrayal.

If Jenna hadn’t been with George, maybe she would’ve conceived with me.
Maybe the pregnancy would’ve gone smoother. Maybe Chloe Dalton wouldn’t have had all those issues as a baby. Maybe we would’ve had more kids.

George wrecked it all the day he seduced my future wife.
And now? He gets to keep seeing her, playing me for a fool, probably laughing behind my back every time they’re together.

No. He wasn’t getting away with this.

As I lay there in bed, I tried to slow my breathing, calm my thoughts.
The anger was boiling under the surface again. I had to chill out, or I’d be up all night.

So I flipped on the TV and found some dumb comedy. One of those shows that makes you laugh even when nothing feels funny.
After a while, it worked. My nerves started to settle, and by 11, I was finally ready to close my eyes.

The next morning, I hit the auction.
Scored some great cars at solid prices and got to talking with a few other dealers in the area.

No Miata showed up at the auction, so I asked around. Two dealers said they had one each—one red, one black.
I told them I’d let them know once I confirmed the buyer’s preference.

Both were eager to make a deal. Said they’d handle the paperwork whenever I was ready.

I gave them the rundown of the cars on my lot, and I couldn’t help but think—I should’ve been coming here more often.
Meeting other folks in the trade, swapping stories, making connections—it felt good.
Helped keep my mind off the mess waiting for me back home.

Later that afternoon, I sat down with the family lawyer my contact had recommended.
The news? Not great.

If I went through with the divorce, I stood to lose half of everything.
On top of that, alimony and child support—for a kid that isn’t even mine.

That was the moment I made a choice.

No divorce. Not yet.

Instead, I’d make sure Jenna and George ended up with each other—and everything that came with it.
I’d pay off the mortgage. Let her live in the house. But since the deed and the loan were in my name alone, she wouldn’t be able to sell it or claim it.
